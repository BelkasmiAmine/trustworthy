\begin{abstract}
We present the main ideas and results of \emph{High-Dimensional Private Empirical Risk Minimization by Greedy Coordinate Descent} (Mangold et al., AISTATS 2023). The paper introduces a differentially private greedy coordinate descent algorithm (DP-GCD) that achieves near-optimal utility in high-dimensional settings. The key insight is that by combining greedy coordinate updates with carefully designed privacy mechanisms, the algorithm attains a logarithmic dependence on the ambient dimension $p$ in favorable regimes. We also discuss its empirical performance and limitations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

We consider empirical risk minimization (ERM):
\[
\min_{w \in \mathbb{R}^p} f(w) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \ell(w, d_i),
\]
under $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-differential privacy.

In high-dimensional settings ($p \gg n$), classical private algorithms such as DP-SGD suffer from utility bounds that scale polynomially in $p$, making them ineffective. Existing methods achieving logarithmic dependence on $p$ typically require constraints (e.g., $\ell_1$ ball), prior knowledge of sparsity, or public data.

The contribution of Mangold et al.\ is an \textbf{unconstrained} algorithm that:
\begin{itemize}
\item achieves $\tilde{O}(\log p)$ dependence,
\item automatically adapts to quasi-sparsity,
\item requires no prior structural knowledge.
\end{itemize}

\section{The DP-GCD Algorithm}

\subsection{Principle}

DP-GCD builds on greedy coordinate descent, which updates one coordinate at a time:
\[
w^{t+1} = w^t - \gamma_j \nabla_j f(w^t) e_j.
\]

The key idea is to:
\begin{itemize}
\item select the most informative coordinate (greedy rule),
\item privatize both the selection and the update.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Algorithm}

Let $M_j$ be coordinate-wise smoothness constants and $L_j$ Lipschitz constants.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{DP-GCD}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize $w_0$
\For{$t = 0$ to $T-1$}
    \State Compute $g_t = \nabla f(w_t)$
    \State $j_t = \arg\max_j \left\{ \frac{|g_{t,j}+ \text{Lap}(\lambda_j) | }{\sqrt{M_j}}  \right\}$
    \State $\tilde{g}_{t,j_t} = g_{t,j_t} + \text{Lap}(\lambda'_{j_t})$
    \State $w_{t+1} = w_t - \frac{1}{M_{j_t}} \tilde{g}_{t,j_t} e_{j_t}$
\EndFor
\State Return $w_T$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Key Insight}

DP-GCD concentrates the privacy budget on a \emph{single coordinate per iteration}, drastically reducing the impact of dimensionality.

\section{Privacy Guarantees}

Each iteration accesses the data twice:
\begin{itemize}
\item coordinate selection (Report-Noisy-Max),
\item noisy gradient computation.
\end{itemize}

\begin{theorem}[Privacy, informal]
If
\[
\lambda_j = \lambda'_j = \Omega\left(\frac{L_j}{n} \frac{\sqrt{T \log(1/\delta)}}{\varepsilon}\right),
\]
then DP-GCD is $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-DP.
\end{theorem}

\paragraph{Idea of proof.}
Each iteration is $(\varepsilon_t, \delta_t)$-DP. Using advanced composition over $2T$ mechanisms yields global $(\varepsilon, \delta)$ privacy.

\section{Utility Analysis}

\subsection{Convex Case}

Define the effective radius:
\[
R_{M,1} = \max_{\substack{w \in \mathbb{R}^p \\ f(w) \le f(w_0)}} \; \max_{w^* \in W^*} \|w - w^*\|_{M,1}.
\]

Then:
\[
f(w_T) - f^* =
\widetilde{O}\left(
R_{M,1}^{4/3} L^{2/3} \varepsilon^{-1/3}
\left(\frac{\log p}{n}\right)^{2/3}
\right).
\]

\subsection{Strongly Convex Case}

\begin{theorem}[Strongly convex utility]
Under component-wise smoothness and $\mu_{M,1}$-strong convexity:
\[
f(w_T) - f^* =
\widetilde{O}\left(
\frac{L^2 \log p}{\mu_{M,1} n^2 \varepsilon^2}
\right).
\]
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Interpretation}

The error decreases at a rate of either \(1/n^{2/3}\) or \(1/n^2\), while the privacy cost scales polynomially in \(1/\varepsilon\). Importantly, the dependence on the dimension \(p\) is only logarithmic.
\section{Quasi-Sparsity}

A vector is quasi-sparse if most coordinates are small.

If $w^*$ has $s$ significant coordinates:
\[
f(w_T) - f^* =
\widetilde{O}\left(
\frac{L^2 s^2 \log p}{\mu n^2 \varepsilon^2}
\right).
\]

\paragraph{Key idea.}
DP-GCD automatically focuses on important coordinates, effectively reducing dimension:
\[
p \rightarrow s \ll p.
\]

\section{Experiments}

\paragraph{Summary.}
We compare three differentially private optimization methods—DP-SGD, DP-CD, and DP-GCD—across different data regimes. Overall, DP-GCD achieves the best performance, especially in high-dimensional sparse settings where \( p \gg n \). In this regime (e.g., dorothea with \( p = 88k \)), DP-GCD is the only method that converges, while DP-SGD and DP-CD fail to significantly decrease the objective.

In quasi-sparse settings (e.g., log2 and madelon), DP-GCD converges approximately \(2\) to \(3\times\) faster and reaches a lower error (around \(10^{-7}\) compared to \(10^{-5}\) for the other methods). In contrast, in low-dimensional problems (e.g., california with \( p = 8 \)), all methods perform similarly, and the additional cost of greedy coordinate selection in DP-GCD is not justified due to the lack of sparsity.

From a computational perspective, one iteration of DP-GCD is equivalent to computing a full gradient (or roughly \( p \) iterations of DP-CD). Despite this higher per-iteration cost, the improved utility per pass makes DP-GCD the only method that remains effective in high-dimensional sparse regimes.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Strengths}

\begin{itemize}
\item No constraint required
\item Logarithmic dependence on $p$
\item Automatic sparsity adaptation
\item Strong empirical performance
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item Full gradient required at each iteration
\item Higher computational cost per step
\item Assumes smoothness
\item Not optimal in all regimes
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

DP-GCD demonstrates that structure-aware private optimization can overcome the curse of dimensionality.

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
By focusing the privacy budget on the most informative coordinates, the algorithm achieves strong utility guarantees in high dimensions.
